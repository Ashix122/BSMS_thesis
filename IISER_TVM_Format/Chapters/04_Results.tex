\chapter{Observations, Results and Challenges}

Critical phenomena in gravitational collapse are most clearly understood in spherical symmetry, where the approach to the critical regime is comparatively straightforward. In this case, the field equations reduce to a system of coupled one-dimensional PDEs, which can be solved with relatively high numerical accuracy. Indeed, in the pioneering work of Matthew Choptuik \cite{choptuik_1993}, the critical parameter was tuned to nearly machine precision, reaching approximately 15 decimal places.

However, once symmetry assumptions are relaxed, the problem becomes significantly more challenging. In the literature, studies of axisymmetric vacuum collapse typically achieve only about 5–7 digits of precision in tuning the critical parameter. This limitation arises from both the increased computational difficulty of solving multidimensional PDEs and the intrinsic nature of critical phenomena, which involve the development of increasingly fine spatial and temporal structures near the threshold of black hole formation.

For the family of initial data considered in this work, the best-tuned critical parameter is found to be 
$p^* = 4.110 \pm 0.003$ for positive values of p and $p^* = -4.110 \pm 0.003$ for negative values of p. Despite this comparatively modest level of tuning, we are able to extract a critical exponent of approximately $\eta \approx 0.354 \pm 0.204$. This suggests that the scaling behavior is reasonably robust even without extremely fine-tuned initial data. In Fig.~\ref{Scaling}, we observe that two families (corresponding to positive and negative values of $p$, as described in Sec.~\ref{Construction}) yield consistent estimates of the critical exponent.We obtain an $R^2$ value of 0.995 for the linear fit to the log--log plot of $K_{\max}$ versus $|p - p^*|$, indicating good agreement with an approximate scaling behaviour.

However, these results should be interpreted with caution. More precise tuning toward the critical threshold, along with a broader set of initial data families, is required before drawing stronger conclusions, particularly regarding universality. This is because at this level of tuning, the system may still be sufficiently far from the critical regime that the extracted exponent is unreliable. Previous studies suggest that insufficient fine-tuning can introduce systematic deviations in the inferred scaling behavior.

The quoted uncertainty in the critical parameter arises from the inability to unambiguously distinguish between subcritical and supercritical evolution within this narrow region of parameter space. In particular, near the threshold, small variations in the parameter lead to qualitatively different outcomes, and within our numerical resolution, this transition cannot be resolved more precisely. This limitation propagates into the determination of the scaling exponent, contributing to the relatively large uncertainty in ($\eta$).

 However, further refinement of the tuning toward the critical regime is required to investigate the critical behavior more reliably, including the possible presence of self-similar structures. For example, as the parameter is tuned closer to the critical value, the evolution exhibits increasingly pronounced oscillatory features in the curvature, suggestive of the onset of echoing behavior as in Fig \ref{feature}. These structures appear as repetitive peaks in the Kretschmann scalar as a function of proper time. However, due to the limited level of tuning and numerical resolution in the present simulations, these features cannot be conclusively identified as true (discrete) self-similarity. Achieving a more definitive verification would require finer tuning of the critical parameter and higher resolution to resolve the increasingly small spacetime scales involved.

\textbf{But what makes tuning and classifying spacetimes difficult?}

As the critical threshold is approached, it becomes increasingly difficult to classify a given evolution as subcritical or supercritical. In particular, near-critical evolutions may:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item not exhibiting a clear dispersal of curvature to spatial infinity,
\item not form an apparent horizon detectable by standard horizon-finding tools,
\end{itemize}
thereby making the outcome ambiguous within finite numerical resolution. This could be attributed to several reasons.

\section*{Development of Coordinate Pathologies}

Often, the inability to classify spacetimes arises from the development of coordinate pathologies, mainly constraint violations and coordinate singularities, in the evolution before we can place them into either supercritical or subcritical data.


\subsection*{Courant Condition and Resolution Limits}

As we approach the critical regime, the solution develops structure on increasingly small spatio-temporal scales. This imposes a fundamental restriction on the timestep via the Courant–Friedrichs–Lewy (CFL) condition, which is required for the stability of explicit numerical schemes.
 For a hyperbolic system with characteristic speed $v_{\max}$, this leads to the condition
\[
\Delta t \leq C \frac{\Delta x}{v_{\max}},
\]
where $C < 1$ is the Courant factor determined by the numerical scheme and $\Delta x$ and $\Delta t$ are the spatial grid spacing and timestep spacing, respectively.


This condition has important consequences near criticality. To resolve the features, \texttt{BAMPS} uses Adaptive Mesh Refinement (AMR), which adjusts the spatial grid sizes to capture dynamics at smaller and smaller scales. As finer spatial resolution ($\Delta x \to 0$) is required to resolve the small-scale features that emerge, the timestep must decrease proportionally to satisfy the CFL condition. Consequently, the computational cost increases significantly, since the number of timesteps required scales inversely with $\Delta t$. With our current computational resources, we were only able to tune to about 3 decimal places.

Additionally, the dynamics of critical phenomena are such that, as we tune towards the threshold of collapse, the evolution resembles each other for an even longer duration, until the solution ``chooses" an end point, be it a singular spacetime or dispersion. So essentially, we will have to evolve more simulation time as we tune the parameter more to reach the critical regime.

Therefore, the combination of shrinking physical scales and the CFL constraint severely limits how close one can tune to the critical parameter in multidimensional simulations. This is one of the primary reasons why achieving high precision in non-symmetric critical collapse is significantly more challenging than in spherical symmetry. This problem can most likely only be mitigated by evolving with more computational resources to effectively resolve the features in a way that doesn't require excessive computational time.

\subsection*{Gauge effects and their resolution}

In near-critical evolutions, coordinate pathologies often manifest as the development of sharp features in the gauge variables, particularly in the shift vector and the lapse. One commonly observed issue is the formation of steep gradients or localized spikes in the shift, which can lead to numerical instability and eventual breakdown of the evolution.

These features are not physical but arise due to the choice of gauge conditions. In particular, certain gauge choices may fail to adequately account for the strong focusing of the geometry near criticality, leading to large coordinate distortions. Since, in NR, the coordinate system (indirectly) evolves alongside spacetime, the gauge dynamics can drive the coordinate system toward pathological configurations. In extreme cases, this can lead to the formation of coordinate singularities during the evolution. Such singularities are purely gauge effects and do not correspond to any physical divergence in the spacetime geometry, but they can nevertheless result in the breakdown of the numerical simulation.

But finding the right gauge is a process that purely involves trial and error, and testing them requires evolving them with multiple gauge choices and understanding how the pathology behaves. Hence, this process takes time, which, in turn, slows down the tuning of the critical parameter.
\section*{Intricacies in Horizon Finding}

Another cause of the inability to classify spacetimes stems from limitations in the AH finders we used. Initially, far away from criticality, we found circular (spherical) AHs, and as we approached the critical regime, we saw more of a ``peanut" shaped horizons as in Fig.\ref{peanut}.  These were comparatively found easily using the spherical parameterized \texttt{AHLoc3D} AH finder from the AH data outputted by \texttt{BAMPS}.

But as we tuned further, the inability to tune the parameter by finding horizons made us rethink the basic assumptions that we had made until now. (Other than the reasons mentioned before, like coordinate singularities and so on). As mentioned in Sec.\ref{horizon}, we hypothesized that the horizon might already be present in the data, but we can't detect it because \texttt{AHLoc3D} uses spherical ray-body parameterization. This turned out to be true, as we were able to find horizons using a Cartesian parameterization-based AH finder \cite{khirnov_2022}, which was modified to account for twist.

    \label{horizon_not}

As we mentioned in Sec.\ref{horizon} and seen in Fig.\ref{horizon_not}, the parameterization is no longer valid for horizons like these, hence the \texttt{AHLoc3D} solver won't converge leading to the inability to find horizons in the data eventhough they are present in the data. Henceforth from now on, we enter the critical regime, we can only rely on this new parameterization. Currently, we find disjoint horizons that are centered around $z=\pm z_{\text{peak}}$ where $z_{\text{peak}}$ is the point on the z axis where the maximum of curvature (Kretschmann Scalar) is located. 


Taken together, these results illustrate that while critical phenomena persist beyond spherical symmetry, accessing and characterizing them demands increasingly sophisticated numerical methods and a deeper understanding of the underlying geometry.
